A quick look at PropAMMs and Solana

August 28, 2023 2-minute read
cryptography • zero-knowledge proofs

PropAMMs are a somewhat new type of automated market maker (AMM) that have become extremely popular in the Solana ecosystem. According to this Dune dashboard, they now amount for up to 75% of trading volume on Solana.

The idea behind them is simple, unlike traditional AMMs like Uniswap or onchain CLOB, the price discovery mechanics and liquidity provisions are abstracted away and taken care off by a proprietary operator. This operator updates the price onchain through frequent cheap transactions to make the price efficient and avoid toxic flow.

That’s the theory, but how does it work in practice?

Case study: BisonFi

According to the Dune dashboard, the two most successful PropAMMs as of January 2026 are HumidiFi and BisonFi. There are already good analyses of HumidiFi available, so let’s look at BisonFi.

The BisonFi program (=smart contract in EVMland) is at address BiSoNHVpsVZW2F7rx2eQ59yQwKxzU5NvBcmKshCSUypi. On Solana, program accounts dont’t hold any state, only compiled code. For each trading pair, there is a market account that holds the state of the pool. We will look at WSOL-USDC and for this pair, the market account is 51FQwjrvo8J8zXUaKyAznJ5NYpoiTCuqAqCu3HAMB9NZ.

So how can we trade this pair? Here is a typical swap Solscan is nice enough to label all the input accounts (that is all the accounts that can be read/modified in the transaction). The instruction data has a pretty standard form:

discriminator (1 byte) + input amount (8 bytes) +
 minimum output amount (8 bytes) + direction (1 byte)

The discriminator is the equivalent to the selector in EVM contracts, i.e., it routes the transaction to a function in the program. So here we can guess that the swap function has discriminator 2. The minimum amount is 0x0065cd1d = 500000000 (LE), which corresponds to 500 USDC since USDC uses 6 decimals. The minimum amount here is just zero, and the direction is 1 meaning the swap is from Token B WSOL to Token A USDC.

This looks easy enough, but how do we know at what price the swap is executed? And how does this price depend on the swap size? And what is the spread?

To answer these questions, we can download the compiled code for the BisonFi program and simulate transactions locally using LiteSVM. To simulate a swap, we also need to carefully set the slot of the simulated transaction. Indeed, BisonFi dismisses swaps with swap_slot > stored_slot + 20, where stored_slot is the slot of the latest price update. This makes sense, since they certainly don’t want people being able to trade at stale prices.
